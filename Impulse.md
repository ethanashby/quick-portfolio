Beginning in the summer of 2019 at the Harvey Mudd Data Science REU, under the mentorship of Professors Johanna Hardin, Daniel Stoebel, and Danae Schulz, I explored the Impulse models (<a href="https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19193146/">Chechik & Koller, Comput. Biol. 16:279–290, 2009</a>), a scaled product of two sigmoidal functions, and its utility in modeling time course RNA-seq data. Time course RNA-seq data violates theoretical assumptions (like independence of samples, since expression values are correlated between time points) that underpin many differential expression algorithms used for analyzing these data. What's more, categorical treatments of time lead to power loss in the ability to detect differentially expressed genes in these experiments.

The model presents a number of advantages to other differential expression methods.

1. The impulse model is a biologically plausible model. The Impulse model can accurately capture transcriptional responses to acute environmental stimuli, while its nested sigmoidal model can capture state-transition/developmental transcriptomic responses.
2. The impulse model treats time as continous, improving power in differential expression analysis as compared to other tools. In fact, the package through which impulse models are fit for differential expression, <a href="https://academic.oup.com/nar/article/46/20/e119/5068248">ImpulseDE2</a>, was determined to be the best performing algorithm in a benchmarking study of time course DE tools (<a href="https://academic.oup.com/bib/article/20/1/288/4364840">Spies, D., et al Brief. Bioinform. 20, 1–11 (2019)</a>).
3. The model is parametrized by biologically meaningful parameters. Particularly the onset time (t1) parameter, which can be used as a proxy for when genes "turn on" during a transcriptional response.

Over the past year, I've conducted methodological inquiry into the model's properties and improving the model fitting procedure. I improved on ImpulseDE2's model fitting procedure by generating many models using random parameter initializations, to avoid local minima fits that weren't descriptive of true expression trends. **You can check out a technical report I wrote on this [here](/ImpulseDE2_modeling_writeup.pdf).**

I'm continuing methodology-based work on this project by doing a simulation study to understand how to better allocate technical replicates and time points in time course RNA-seq experiments. The goal of this work is to inform the design of time course RNA-seq experiments to obtain better estimation of the onset time parameter.

## Application to time course RNA-seq data for *E. coli* under cell starvation

RpoS is an alternative sigma factor that controls the stress response of *E. coli* bacteria. RpoS accumulates in the cell when it is exposed to a stressor, and genes in the RpoS regulon are then transcribed to coordinate the cell's physiological response. Consequently, levels of RpoS vary continuously in the cell throughout the course of exposure to a stressor and also between different stressors, as some upregulate RpoS more strongly than others. Previous research in the Stoebel lab at Harvey-Mudd College has investigated how genes respond to varying levels of RpoS and has found three distinct types of responses: Linear, where expression changes linearly with increasing levels of RpoS, Sensitive, which increase sharply in response to a small amount of RpoS; and Insensitive, which increase only a little in response to the same small amount of RpoS. These classes of responses were identified in cells entering stationary phase, a period of reduced growth and starvation reached naturally by bacterial populations growing in the lab. It has been found that, in stationary phase, RpoS influences the expression of over 1,000 genes, which is 23% of the e. Coli genome.

In the summer of 2019, I identified the potential of sigmoidal and impulse model in modeling *E. coli* gene expression responses to environmental stimuli, which has become a major research interest of the Stoebel lab. In conjunction with Professors Dan Stoebel (Harvey-Mudd College), Johanna Hardin (Pomona College), and students in the Stoebel lab, I conducted differential expression analyses to identify differentially expressed genes, fit sigmoidal/impulse models to significant genes, extracted the "onset time" parameters, and ran hypothesis tests on these parameters. This analysis demonstrated that RpoS-Sensitive genes turn on approximately 20 minutes earlier than RpoS-Insensitive genes in cell starvation, suggesting that RpoS-sensitivity may coordinate gene expression timing. This was not observed in other stress types, suggesting sensitivity is a means of coordinating a stress-specific response, instead of conserved feature of all RpoS-mediated stress responses.

In collaboration with Emily Petroni (Harvey-Mudd College Department of Biology), we presented this work at the The 12th Annual Undergraduate Research Conference at the Interface of Biology and Mathematics, run by the National Institute of Mathematical and Biological Synthesis (NIMBios): **You can check out our slide presentation [here](/NIMBios 2020.pdf).**

## Application to time course RNA-seq data for *T. brucei* under bromodomain protein knockout

West African Trypanosomiasis, commonly known as sleeping sickness, is a disease with devastating health and economic consequences: several hundred thousand infections occur annually and if left untreated, it can result in death. Infections are transmitted by the bite of a Tsetse fly carrying the protozoan parasite *Trypanosoma brucei* (*T. brucei*). During its life cycle, the parasite modulates its transcriptomic response to respond to the different environments in the host's body. Two general life cycle forms that the parasite takes are procyclic form and bloodstream form. In T.brucei bloodstream form, the parasite becomes densely covered with highly immunogenic variant surface glycoproteins (VSGs), allowing it to evade the mammalian antibody immune response.
In mammals, histone acetyl-lysine recognition by **bromodomain proteins** are known to control stem cell fate. Previous work by Professor Danae Schulz showed that bromodomain proteins are responsible for the maintenance of *T. brucei* bloodstream form stage identity. In collaboration with Professor Schulz at Harvey-Mudd college, we analyzed an RNA-Seq dataset to determine the role of bromodomain proteins in *procyclic-form T. brucei*. I developed a differential expression pipeline and a fuzzy c-means clustering framework to identify core groups of differentially expressed genes in WT procylic form compared to a bromodomain-knockout strain. Professor Schulz will cross-reference these clusters with known *T. brucei* development programmes, to understand what the impact of bromodomain proteins on parasitic life cycle in the procylic phase. **Manuscript coming soon!**
